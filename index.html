Everything I Tried. Everything That Failed.

My name is Karen. I'm 47. I live in Brisbane with my husband and our two kids.

My neck pain started after a minor car accident. Nothing dramatic — a small rear-end collision in a car park. The whiplash was real but mild. My GP said it would settle in 6–8 weeks.

It didn't.

Over the next four years I tried everything I was told to try:

  • Physiotherapy — twice a week for 6 months. Felt better during sessions. Back to square one within 48 hours.
  • Remedial massage — monthly, then fortnightly. Two hours of relief, then same pain again.
  • Ibuprofen — took it so often my stomach started protesting. Stopped working after a while anyway.
  • Heat packs every night — helped me fall asleep. Woke up stiff and sore every morning regardless.
  • A specialist chiropractor — $180 a session. Six sessions. Zero lasting change.
  • Acupuncture — gave it a genuine go. Felt odd. Did nothing for my neck.

I wasn't lazy about it. I wasn't a bad patient. I showed up, I did the exercises, I followed the advice.

The pain just kept coming back.

And nobody ever explained why.

The Thing Nobody Ever Told Me

The answer came from the most unlikely place — an offhand comment from a physio I saw once for a second opinion.

She was looking at my notes and said, almost to herself: "After this long, it's probably not the muscle anymore. It's likely nerve sensitisation."

I asked her to explain that.

She stopped what she was doing, sat down, and said: "Your original injury healed. Or mostly healed. But the nerve pathway that was sending pain signals during the injury — it got stuck. It's still firing. Even though there's nothing to warn you about anymore, the signal keeps going. Your brain keeps receiving pain because the pathway never switched off."

She called it a "pain loop."

That one sentence explained four years of my life.

Here is the part that made me genuinely angry:

Every single treatment I had tried — physio stretches, massage, heat, anti-inflammatories — works on the muscle layer. Which is why they give temporary relief. The muscle relaxes. But the nerve pathway underneath? Still firing. Still looping. Still sending the signal.

Nobody was reaching the actual cause. They were treating a wound that had already closed, while the alarm system inside kept screaming.

Research suggests this pattern — called central sensitisation — affects up to 70% of people with chronic neck pain that lasts more than 3 months. Most of them never find out that's what it is.

I wasn't broken. I wasn't imagining it. I wasn't "just stressed."

My nervous system had learned to stay in pain mode. And nothing I had tried was designed to reach it.
