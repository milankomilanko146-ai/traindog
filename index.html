What Nobody in the Clinic Tells You About Chronic Pain

Here's what the neuroscience research has known for over two decades, but which almost never makes it into a GP's office or a standard physio session:

Chronic neck pain is usually not a muscle problem.

It starts as one. You strain something. You sleep at a bad angle. Stress causes your trapezius to seize up. Normal. The muscle gets inflamed, it hurts, you treat it.

But then something goes wrong in the recovery process.

The nerve pathways that were carrying pain signals during the injury get "stuck" in an active state. The original injury heals — or at least, it heals enough. But the nerves keep firing.

Scientists call this central sensitisation — but a simpler way to understand it is this: your nervous system has been trained to expect pain, so it keeps producing it. Even when the original damage is gone.

This is what the research shows happens in an estimated 60–80% of chronic neck pain cases in women over 40.

Your brain, in a misguided attempt to protect you, has created a feedback loop that has taken on a life of its own.

This is why massage gives you two hours of relief and then the pain is back. The therapist relaxed the muscle — but the nerve signal firing away in the background was never touched.

This is why heat helps temporarily. Heat increases circulation and triggers endorphin release — which quiets the signal for a while. But the loop restarts.

This is why painkillers stop working. They don't interrupt the loop. They just lower your perception of the signal for a few hours.

None of the conventional treatments — not one — is designed to reach the nerve pathway and reset it.

Why Your Instincts Were Right All Along

Here's what I want you to hear clearly:

You are not imagining the pain. You are not exaggerating. You are not "just stressed."

The pain is neurologically real. It is being actively generated by your nervous system. The fact that scans look "normal" doesn't mean there's nothing wrong — it means the problem isn't structural. It's functional. It's electrical.

And that distinction matters enormously. Because structural problems — herniated discs, bone spurs — are hard to reverse. Functional nerve problems can be interrupted.

When I understood this, I started looking for anything that had been clinically shown to actually reach the nerve pathway level — not just the muscle layer.

What the research showed me about conventional approaches:

Massage therapy: Effectively relaxes superficial and deep muscle tissue. Does not reach the nerve pathway. Pain typically returns within 24–72 hours for chronic sufferers.
Heat therapy: Triggers temporary endorphin response and improves local circulation. No sustained effect on sensitised nerve pathways.
NSAIDs (ibuprofen etc.): Reduce inflammation in acute injury. Largely ineffective for central sensitisation, and carry well-documented risks with long-term use.
Physio exercises: Excellent for rebuilding strength and improving posture. Address the contributing factors but cannot directly reset hyperactive pain pathways.

None of these are useless. But none of them are reaching the root cause in chronic cases.
